How Search Engines Work

Search engines have two major functions: (Fishkin, 2015)

  1. Crawling and building an index
  2. Providing users with a ranked list of websites

Crawling and Indexing

The search engines have automated robots called crawlers or spiders to scan the net using links and store title and meta data from each page in an indexed database. These databases are distributed over hundreds of machines located all over the world. (Fishkin, 2015)

Ranking Search Results

Hundreds of factors influence the ranking of web pages in search results. Here are the most influential: (Fishkin, 2015)

  1. Matched words in the index. Most search engines index title and content words other than articles such as "a", "and" and "the." Google gives more weight to words in the title and meta tags. (Franklin, 2000) Ranking is calculated based on the frequency and location of the keywords. (Strickland, 2006)
  2. How long the page has been on the web
  3. Engagement Metrics - how long a user stays at a site before trying the next one in the ranking
  4. Algorithms than judge site quality modeled after human assessments
  5. Number of links to the site from highly ranked sites

Search Engine Optimization (SEO)

Search engine optimization (SEO) is the process of improvind the ranking of a website or a web page in a search engine's unpaid results. Legitimate means of doing this include: (Strickland, 2008)

  • Having good content.
  • Using keywords in the page titles, head section, and throughout the document.
  • Exchanging links with related web sites

Keyword and Description Meta Tags

The keyword meta tags appear in the head of html documents and are used to provide web crawlers with key words for their algorithms to use. The webcrawlers compare these keywords to the content of the document to prevent sites from being listed inappropriately. (Strickland, 2008)

The description meta tags also appear in the head and provide a short description or summary of the contents of the page. Google Webmaster Tools provides a handy content analysis section that reveals description meta tags that are either too short or too long. (Google, 2010)
